How a build comes together

Find the person, not the problem

Every build starts by identifying someone with a recurring friction — a driver, a mechanic, a student — before any sketch happens. The constraint comes from them, not from what's fun to model.

Constrain it like an engineer

Cost, manufacturability, and the materials someone could actually source matter as much as performance. A solution nobody can build or afford isn't a solution.

Model, simulate, stress-test

CAD geometry, load cases, and tolerance checks come next — testing the idea against physics before it's tested against an audience.

Hand it back to the person it's for

The final measure of a build isn't how it looks rendered — it's whether the person it was designed for would actually want to use it.

ABOUT ME

Menuka Ratnajeewa

I'm a Mechanical Engineering student at the University of Birmingham, originally based in Dubai. Cars and the machines that move people have been my way into engineering since before I knew the word "torque" — and that pull is still what gets me into a CAD model at midnight.

What keeps me there is the question of who's on the other end of the design. A faster gearbox is interesting. A gearbox that lets a specific person drive again, commute more safely, or fix their own car for less — that's the project I actually want to finish.

This page collects that work: real constraints, real people in mind, and the engineering behind getting from idea to something that could exist.
